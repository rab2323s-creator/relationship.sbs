Trauma Bond Test Results — Which Attachment Pattern Are You In?

Understanding Trauma Bonding, Emotional Addiction, and Push–Pull Cycles

If you’re here, you’re probably not looking for a label—you’re looking for clarity. Trauma bonding isn’t “being dramatic.” It’s what happens when your nervous system learns a pattern: distress → relief → hope reset. The relief feels powerful, and the bond deepens—not because it’s safe, but because it’s unpredictable.

The 5 Trauma Bond Result Profiles

Choose the profile that matches your result, or read them in order to understand how attachment can shift from stable → intense → unstable cycles.

  1. Secure Attachment Dynamic
    Stability is the baseline. Repair creates real change—not temporary “good phases.”
  2. Intense Attachment Pattern
    Big feelings, but you still have footing. Anxiety shows up, yet you can reality-check and self-soothe.
  3. Push–Pull Relationship Cycle
    Hot-and-cold closeness trains your attention. You chase the “good version” and brace for the drop.
  4. Emotional Addiction Loop
    Relief starts to feel like oxygen. Silence triggers withdrawal-like distress and compulsive checking.
  5. Trauma Bond Pattern
    Pain + relief can create a powerful pull. Boundaries may trigger consequences, and leaving can feel unbearable.

What These Results Actually Mean

These profiles don’t measure “how much you love someone.” They measure patterns: consistency, repair after conflict, boundary responses, silence/withdrawal, and whether closeness is something you can count on—or something you have to earn.

Trauma bond vs. push–pull vs. healthy love

A push–pull cycle often feels like closeness followed by sudden distance with no clarity. A trauma bond usually intensifies the pull through intermittent reinforcement— warmth appears at key moments (often after distress), making your body chase relief. Healthy love is different: repair leads to stable behavior, and you don’t have to shrink to keep peace.

Best Next Steps (Simple, Practical)

  • Look for repetition: track the cycle over weeks, not moments.
  • Measure consistency: not intensity, apologies, or chemistry.
  • Protect your nervous system: pause before reacting during peak anxiety.
  • Set one boundary you can keep: then follow through once.

If your result points to high distress, the fastest clarity often comes from mapping patterns and rebuilding support outside the relationship.
