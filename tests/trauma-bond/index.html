Trauma bonding doesn’t always look dramatic. It often looks like intense closeness followed by distance, warmth followed by silence, and hope followed by confusion. Over time, your nervous system can learn to chase relief instead of stability—because relief feels like the reward.

This is why terms like trauma bond test, intermittent reinforcement relationship, and emotional addiction in relationships are searched so often: people want a clear way to name the pattern—without blaming themselves or labeling someone else.

Trauma Bond vs. Push–Pull vs. Healthy Love (Quick Clarity)

A push–pull cycle can happen when closeness triggers fear and distance triggers longing—creating repeated resets. A trauma bond often intensifies that pull through intermittent reinforcement: warmth appears unpredictably (often after distress), which can make the bond feel addictive. In healthy love, repair leads to consistent behavior—not a temporary “good phase.”

This quiz focuses on patterns over moments: what repeats, what predicts the next drop, and how your body responds (calm, vigilance, relief, or withdrawal). You’ll get a profile that explains what may be driving the attachment.

FAQ

What is a trauma bond in a relationship?

A trauma bond is a strong emotional attachment shaped by repeated cycles of distress and relief. It often includes intermittent reinforcement and difficulty leaving despite harm.

What is intermittent reinforcement in relationships?

It means affection, attention, or reassurance appears unpredictably—sometimes intensely—then disappears. Unpredictability can strengthen attachment because your nervous system starts chasing the next “good” moment.

Is trauma bonding the same as love?

Not necessarily. Healthy love tends to feel stable, safe, and consistent. Trauma bonding often feels intense, anxiety-driven, and dependent on relief after distress.

Is this trauma bond test a diagnosis?

No. This is an educational self-reflection quiz about patterns. It does not diagnose a person or mental health condition and does not replace professional support.

Why can’t I leave a toxic relationship?

Many people struggle to leave when the relationship includes powerful relief moments after distress. This can create hope resets and emotional cravings. Support and clear boundaries can help you regain clarity.
